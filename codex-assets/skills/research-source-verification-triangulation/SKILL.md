# research-source-verification-triangulation

Trace every load-bearing claim to its **primary source** — the original study, dataset, statute, or first-hand record — not a news write-up or a review citing it. Citation chains drift; the secondary source often misstates the primary. Verify the source exists and says what's claimed (open the actual paper/page, check the quoted number in context).

**Read laterally** (the fact-checker move): leave the source and open new tabs to see what independent others say about it, rather than evaluating it on its own polished surface. Run a **CRAAP** scan (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) on each source, weighting Authority (who, what credentials, what incentive) and Purpose (what are they selling). For academic sources, check **Retraction Watch** / the journal for retractions or expressions of concern before relying on a paper.

**Triangulate**: corroborate a finding across independent sources (**data triangulation**), methods (**methodological**), and analysts (**investigator triangulation**) — convergence raises confidence, divergence flags a problem to explain.

Pitfall: laundering an unverified claim because it appeared in many places that all copied one origin (circular sourcing). Trace to the root. Second pitfall: treating a preprint or a press release as a peer-reviewed result.

**Tools:** lateral reading, CRAAP test, primary-source tracing, Retraction Watch, triangulation (data/method/investigator/theory), DOI/archive checks
