# ba-financial-statement-analysis

Read the three statements as a linked system: P&L shows accrual profit, balance sheet shows position at a point, cash-flow shows actual cash and bridges the two. Build the core ratio set: profitability (gross / operating(EBIT) / net margin, EBITDA margin, ROE = DuPont: net margin × asset turnover × equity multiplier, ROA, ROIC vs WACC), liquidity (current = CA/CL, quick = (CA−inventory)/CL), leverage (debt/equity, net debt/EBITDA, interest coverage = EBIT/interest), efficiency (DSO, DIO, DPO → cash conversion cycle = DSO+DIO−DPO). Do common-size analysis (every P&L line as % of revenue, every BS line as % of assets) to compare across size and time, plus horizontal/trend (YoY growth, CAGR). Expert move: reconcile net income to operating cash flow — a persistent gap (rising income, flat/negative CFO) is the classic accruals red flag for channel-stuffing or aggressive revenue recognition; also watch DSO creeping up faster than sales. Distinguish operating vs investing vs financing cash — 'profit' funded by debt or asset sales isn't sustainable. Pitfalls: comparing ratios across industries (capital intensity differs); one-period snapshots (always trend + benchmark to peers); ignoring off-balance-sheet leases/obligations; EBITDA treated as cash (it ignores capex and working-capital drag).

**Tools:** Excel, financial modeling, ratio analysis
