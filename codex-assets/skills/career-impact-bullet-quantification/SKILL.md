# career-impact-bullet-quantification

Quantification is the single biggest lever on bullet quality. Every claim needs a **number that signals scope or a delta that signals improvement.** Four metric families: (1) performance — latency, throughput, QPS, p99, build time; (2) scale — users, requests/day, data volume, services, team size; (3) money — revenue, cost saved, infra spend cut; (4) time — hours saved/week, cycle-time, deploy frequency.

When you 'have no numbers,' you have proxies. Reconstruct the baseline-vs-after delta: before/after a refactor, count incidents, on-call pages, or test runtime. Don't have exact figures? Estimate defensibly and round conservatively (`~40% fewer support tickets`). Scope quantifiers work too: `across 12 microservices`, `for a 5-engineer team`, `processing 4TB/day`.

**What top candidates do differently:** they pair a metric with the *mechanism* (the Z in XYZ) so an interviewer can ask a real follow-up — the bullet becomes a story seed, not a dead end. They also tie engineering work to business outcomes (`cut churn`, `unblocked launch`).

**Common mistake:** inflating to round, suspiciously clean numbers (`improved performance by 1000%`) you can't defend in the interview, or quantifying trivia (`wrote 5,000 lines of code` — volume ≠ value). Every number must survive a 'how did you measure that?' probe.

**Tools:** XYZ/CAR formula, baseline-delta framing, proxy metrics, scope quantifiers
